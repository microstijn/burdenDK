# Does `g`-driven vulnerability map onto real biology? (checks #1 and #2)

*Working note. Companion to `lambda_min_maintenance_rate.tex`. After re-anchoring
`λ_min` to `k_M`, the amplification index `F` tracks the energy investment ratio
`g`. Before chasing scarce chronic-then-acute data, two cheap checks: (#1) is the
*direction* biologically arguable, and (#2) does the ordering line up with any
recognized life-history axis? Generated by `examples/amp_g_lifehistory_check.jl`.*

## #2 — `g` vs AmP life-history traits (Spearman, 7,335 species)

| trait (axis) | ρ(g, trait) | ρ(F@0.5, trait) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| assimilation `{p_Am}` (intake) | **−0.920** | −0.653 |
| maintenance rate `k_M` (metab) | −0.759 | −0.474 |
| von Bertalanffy rate `r_B` (fast) | −0.530 | −0.319 |
| age at puberty `a_p` (slow) | +0.382 | +0.234 |
| ultimate length `L_i` (size) | +0.261 | +0.075 |
| life span `a_m` (slow) | −0.185 | −0.209 |
| ultimate wet weight `Ww_i` (size) | −0.078 | −0.148 |

Reference: ρ(g, κ) = −0.244.

### Reading it (two cautions that flip the headline)

1. **The strong correlations are algebraic self-reference, not validation.**
   `g = [E_G]·v / (κ·{p_Am})`, so `g ∝ 1/{p_Am}` by construction. The ρ = −0.92
   against assimilation is therefore *definitional*, and `k_M`, `r_B` are likewise
   derived from the same primary DEB parameters. These are not independent traits.

2. **Against the genuinely emergent traits — body size and life span — `g` is weak
   to orthogonal** (|ρ| ≤ 0.26). So the model's "vulnerable" species are **not** a
   recognizable size class or pace-of-life class.

### The uncomfortable reduction

Stripped of the self-referential traits, `g`'s dominant empirical content is
**`g ≈ 1/{p_Am}`** — i.e. the model says *low-assimilation species are vulnerable*.
That is nearly as reductive as the original "vulnerability = κ": we have replaced
one primary DEB parameter (allocation κ) with another (inverse assimilation
`{p_Am}`). **The one-dimensional `F`-index keeps collapsing onto a single primary
parameter** — which points at the *dimensionality* of `F = λ(A₀)/λ(A_t)`, not the
choice of `λ_min`, as the real limitation.

## #1 — is the direction biologically arguable?

The clean mechanistic story **for** high-`g` = vulnerable: `g` high means the
reserve buffer is small relative to the cost of structure, so a chronically-drawn-
down organism has little reserve to mobilize for recovery → weaker restoring force
→ amplified acute burden. Reserve is the buffer; reserve-poor is fragile. Coherent.

The argument **against**: once you see `g ≈ 1/{p_Am}`, the claim becomes
"low surface-specific assimilation → vulnerable." That is not a standard or
compelling vulnerability narrative — low-throughput species are often *more*
robust (lower demand, can coast), so the direction may be backwards or simply not
meaningful. And 65% of species (`g ≤ 1`) are declared *immune* (`F = 1`) at a
boundary with no obvious biological meaning.

## Verdict

Neither cheap check supports `g` as a real vulnerability axis: it is orthogonal to
the emergent life-history traits, and its dominant signal reduces to inverse
assimilation. Combined with the structural one-dimensionality of `F`, the honest
conclusion is that **the single-ratio amplification index — even with a correct
`λ_min` — is unlikely to encode real multi-dimensional vulnerability biology.**
The next move is not another internal re-anchoring; it is (a) an external,
empirical chronic-then-acute anchor and DEB-expert review, and/or (b) reconsidering
whether `F`-as-a-scalar-ratio is the right output at all.
